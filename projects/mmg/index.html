Overview

MMG answers a simple question with unusual rigor: which Old School RuneScape money-making methods and Grand Exchange flips are actually worth it right now? A live, fully client-side web app computes deterministic profit margins from real-time OSRS Wiki price data. A separate research track asks the harder question: can short-horizon price movement be predicted at all?

Architecture

Three loosely coupled pieces: a static frontend (live on Cloudflare Pages) whose analysis pages run entirely in the browser against the OSRS Wiki real-time prices API, a local-first Python data collector and ML backend for the research track, and a small accounts microservice on a cloud VM. The public demo needs no backend at all. That's a deliberate design choice, one that keeps the useful part free, fast, and always up.

Research methodology

The ML track is built like a research program, not a trading bot: exact-horizon targets, walk-forward evaluation with purge and embargo to prevent leakage, pre-registered decision criteria, and saved evaluation artifacts. The headline result so far is an honest negative. The evaluated models did not clear the bar for a live recommendation, and the reports say so plainly. That discipline (don't promote a weak result into a product claim) is the point of the project.

Try it

The margin analyzer and flip finder are live and free. No account, no install. The research reports and code are on GitHub.

The phantom-spread debunk: what happens when you charge realistic fills

Same model signals, 30-day out-of-sample walk-forward, three different assumptions about which side of the spread a fill actually lands on. The net swings from a 22.9M gp "profit" to a 22.6M gp loss without changing the model at all, only the fill assumption. Two naive baselines (EMA crossover, random entry) are included for scale, under the same optimistic fills.

Objective reframe: taker vs. market-maker (the alternative that was rejected, and why)

The taker framing (buy the ask, dump the bid, a 2.2% positive rate) forces a class weight of roughly 20 to one that the calibrator can't rescue, negative Brier skill, high AUC that doesn't translate into usable probabilities. Reframing the same features around posting both sides of the spread (a 31.8% positive rate, closer to balanced) fixes calibration without changing what the model can see.
