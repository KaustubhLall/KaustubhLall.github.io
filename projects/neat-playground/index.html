What it is

NEAT evolves both the weights and the topology of small neural networks. This playground implements the whole algorithm from scratch in C99 and points it at four arcade games: Flappy Bird, Snake, 2048, and Pac-Man. There is no training data and no gradient descent. Populations of agents play, the best reproduce, and structure grows only when it earns its keep.

Why C, and what the speed buys

The simulation core runs on the order of tens of millions of agent-steps per second per core. That throughput turns neuroevolution from an overnight job into an interactive loop: change a fitness function, rerun evolution, and watch the behavioral difference minutes later. A full experimental report and the evolution histories live in the repo.

The visualizers

Each game has a browser replay visualizer that loads a committed evolution history and plays it back: the game on one side, the evolved network on the other, sensors and activations updating live. They are hosted on this site, so you can watch what each generation learned without installing anything.

Results and ablations

Each game tells a different story. Flappy Bird is a reflex task and gets solved by generation 16 with a network of about 10 nodes. Snake climbs steadily to 51 apples as path planning develops. 2048 shows the most interesting curve: roughly 120 generations of plateau, then a sharp grokking jump around generation 170 to 200. Pac-Man rises fast and stays noisy because ghost encounters add variance.

The ablations are the point, not just the scores. Training on a single fixed world collapses on unseen seeds, which pins down memorization. Recurrent connections nearly double 2048 performance and do nothing for Flappy Bird. Population diversity helps the reflex game and hurts the planning games. Every world is procedurally generated and never reused, so doing well on training worlds is itself evidence of generalization, and the whole study reproduces bit-for-bit from one seed.

Architecture and results

Architecture

One game-agnostic NEAT engine (2,086 lines across 7 files) is driven by three binaries (trainer, viewer, server). All three reach every game through the same Env vtable defined in include/env.h, four calls (observe, step, reset, score) that each of the four game modules (1,238 lines total) implements independently. Adding a fifth game means writing one new file behind that vtable, not touching the engine or the drivers.

Memorization ablation

Same population, two training regimes: one world reused for the whole run ("fixed world") vs. a new random world every generation ("random world"), both then scored on a world neither ever saw. Hollow bars are the score on the world the population actually trained on; solid cyan bars are the score on an unseen world. Flappy and 2048 show the memorization gap most sharply: fixed-world 2048 scores 128 on its own training world but collapses to 6 on an unseen one, while random-world training holds at 64 and 42. Snake's random-world unseen score (25) even edges past its own random-world training score (12), reflecting how noisy a single-run comparison at this scale is.
